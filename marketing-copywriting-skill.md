# Marketing Copywriting

**Write clear, specific, credible marketing copy that earns trust and drives action — for any page on your website.**

---

## What This Skill Does

This skill helps you write or improve marketing copy for any page of a website: homepages, landing pages, pricing pages, feature pages, about pages, and use-case or audience-specific pages. It covers headlines, subheadlines, body copy, CTAs, and page structure — with principles that apply whether you're writing your first product page or rewriting one that isn't converting.

The approach prioritizes clarity and credibility over cleverness. Every claim should be specific enough to be believed, and every section should move the reader toward one clear action.

---

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when you want to:

- Write copy for a new or existing web page
- Improve a page that isn't converting
- Sharpen a value proposition, headline, or tagline
- Make copy more specific, more credible, or easier to read
- Structure a page to guide visitors toward a single action

## Before Writing

Gather what isn't already obvious. Don't start writing without answers to:

| Input | Questions to answer |
|-------|-------------------|
| Page purpose | What type of page is this? What is the ONE action you want visitors to take? |
| Audience | Who is this for? What problem are they solving? What objections or skepticism do they bring? What words do *they* use for the problem? |
| Product / offer | What is being offered? What makes it different? What's the key transformation or outcome? What proof exists (numbers, case studies, certifications, testimonials)? |
| Traffic context | Where is traffic coming from — ad, organic search, email, referral? What does the visitor already know when they land? |

## Core Principles

Apply these in every piece of copy you write or review:

**1. Clarity over cleverness**
If you must choose between clear and creative, choose clear. A visitor who doesn't immediately understand what you do will leave.

**2. Benefits over features**
Features describe what a product does. Benefits describe what that means for the reader. State both: the feature and its payoff.
- Weak: "Real-time collaboration"
- Strong: "Everyone works from the same version — no more conflicting edits or stale files"

**3. Specificity over vagueness**
Specific claims are more persuasive and more credible than general ones.
- Weak: "Save time on your workflow"
- Strong: "Cut your weekly reporting from four hours to fifteen minutes"
- Avoid: "streamline," "optimize," "seamless," "innovative," "powerful," "robust"

**4. Customer language over company language**
Use words your customers use — drawn from reviews, interviews, and support conversations — not internal jargon or buzzwords.

**5. One idea per section**
Each section advances one argument. The page is a logical sequence, not a catalog of everything you could say.

**6. Honest over sensational**
Vague superlatives ("the best," "the leading," "world-class") erode trust. Fabricated or unsupported statistics create legal and reputational risk. Only claim what you can substantiate, and put the evidence next to the claim.

## Style Rules

Apply these to every draft before delivering:

- **Simple words:** "use" not "utilize," "help" not "facilitate," "show" not "demonstrate"
- **Active voice:** "We generate the report" not "The report is generated"
- **Confident:** Remove "almost," "very," "really," "quite," "sort of"
- **Show, don't tell:** Describe the outcome instead of piling on adverbs ("saves ten hours a week" beats "incredibly efficient")
- **No exclamation points:** They undermine credibility; remove them
- **No buzzwords without substance:** If a word could describe any company in any industry, cut it

**Pre-ship quality check:**
- Any jargon an outsider wouldn't immediately understand?
- Sentences trying to do more than one thing?
- Passive voice constructions?
- Any claims without evidence right next to them?
- Any superlatives that aren't substantiated?

## Page Structure Framework

### Above the Fold

Every page needs three elements above the scroll:

**Headline — your single most important message**
- Communicates the core value proposition in one line
- Specific beats generic; concrete beats abstract
- Formulas that work:
  - "{Achieve outcome} without {pain point}"
  - "The {category} for {specific audience}"
  - "Never {unpleasant event} again"
  - "{Question that names the main pain}"
  - "{Specific outcome} in {timeframe or condition}"

**Subheadline — expands the headline**
- Adds specificity or addresses a key objection
- 1–2 sentences maximum
- Should not repeat the headline — extend it

**Primary CTA — action + what they get**
- "Start free trial" beats "Sign up"
- "Get the checklist" beats "Download"
- "See pricing for my team" beats "Learn more"
- Formula: [Action verb] + [What they get] + [Optional qualifier]

### Core Page Sections

Sequence these to fit the audience and traffic source:

| Section | Purpose | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Social proof | Build credibility early | Logos, review scores, customer counts, named testimonials |
| Problem / pain | Show you understand the situation | Use the customer's language, not your diagnosis |
| Solution / benefits | Connect features to 3–5 outcomes | Each benefit: feature → what it does → what that means for them |
| How it works | Reduce perceived complexity | 3–4 steps maximum; make it look simple and achievable |
| Objection handling | Address hesitation | FAQ, comparison, guarantee, risk reversal |
| Final CTA | Close the page | Restate the value, repeat the CTA, reduce the risk of action |

## Page-Specific Guidance

### Homepage
- Serves multiple audiences — lead with the broadest true value, then give clear paths for different intents (e.g., "For teams" / "For individuals")
- Every visitor type should find their path within the first scroll
- The homepage is often the first and only impression — make the value proposition undeniably clear

### Landing Page
- One message, one CTA — remove navigation if the traffic source is paid
- Match the headline precisely to the ad or email that sent the visitor here
- Make the complete argument on one page; don't assume they'll click to learn more

### Pricing Page
- Help visitors choose the right plan, not just compare options
- Make the recommended plan visually obvious
- Address "which plan is right for me?" anxiety directly
- Name and answer price objections: "no credit card required," "cancel anytime," "includes everything you need to get started"
- Show what changes between plans, not just what's included

### Feature Page
- Connect: feature → what it does → benefit → outcome
- Include use cases and concrete examples for each feature
- Provide a clear path to try or buy at the bottom

### About Page
- Tell the story of why you exist — not a corporate bio
- Connect the mission to a real customer benefit
- Include a CTA — "About" pages have more intent than most people assume

### Use-Case / "Who It's For" Page
- Write entirely in the target segment's language
- Structure: their trigger, their pain, how you solve it, their outcome, proof relevant to them, objections, CTA
- One page per audience — don't try to serve everyone on one page

## Headlines and CTAs in Depth

### Headline evaluation checklist
- [ ] Can a stranger understand what this company does from the headline alone?
- [ ] Is it specific? (Does it contain a concrete outcome, number, or detail?)
- [ ] Is it benefit-led? (Does it say what's in it for the reader?)
- [ ] Is it active voice?
- [ ] Does it avoid vague superlatives and buzzwords?
- [ ] Does it avoid clickbait the page can't deliver on?

### CTA evaluation checklist
- [ ] Does the button text say what happens when they click?
- [ ] Is the action verb strong and clear?
- [ ] Does it reduce perceived risk where possible?
- [ ] Is there one primary CTA — not two or three competing for attention?

**Weak CTAs to avoid:** Submit, Sign Up, Learn More, Click Here, Get Started (too vague), Continue

**Strong CTA patterns:**
- "Start my free 14-day trial"
- "Get the free report"
- "See pricing for my team size"
- "Book a 20-minute demo"
- "Request early access"
- "Create your first [thing]"

---

## Quality Checks and Guardrails

Before delivering any copy:

- [ ] Every claim has evidence next to it, or is clearly framed as an opinion
- [ ] No unsupported superlatives ("best," "leading," "world-class") appear without proof
- [ ] There is one — and only one — primary CTA per page
- [ ] The headline communicates the value proposition in plain language
- [ ] The copy uses customer vocabulary (from research or interviews), not company jargon
- [ ] All statistics are sourced or noted as estimates
- [ ] The reading level is appropriate for the audience (use short words and short sentences by default)
- [ ] The page tells a logical story — each section leads to the next
- [ ] Alternatives are offered for the headline (2–3 options) and primary CTA (2 options)
