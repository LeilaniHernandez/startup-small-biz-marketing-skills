# Competitor Profiling

**Research and profile competitors into structured, comparable documents that inform positioning, messaging, comparison pages, and strategy.**

---

## What This Skill Does

This skill turns a list of competitor URLs into clear, structured competitor profiles and a cross-competitor summary. The output is decision-useful intelligence: every claim traced to a source, all profiles using the same template so they compare cleanly side by side, and an honest assessment of where each rival is strong and where they're not.

This skill is **tool-agnostic**. It works with standard web search and page access. If specialized SEO or backlink tools are available, they add quantitative depth — but they're accelerants, not requirements. Never block progress on a tool you don't have.

---

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when you want to:

- Understand your competitive landscape before a launch or repositioning
- Build comparison or alternative pages for your website
- Sharpen your positioning relative to specific rivals
- Understand how competitors are messaging and to whom
- Find gaps in the market that represent positioning opportunities
- Prepare a competitive intelligence briefing for a team or investor

**Trigger phrases:** "competitor research," "competitor profile," "competitive analysis," "competitive intelligence," "profile this competitor," "who are my competitors," "competitor landscape," "competitive audit," "compare to competitors," "how do competitors position themselves"

---

## Inputs to Confirm

Before starting, establish:

1. **Competitor URLs** — the list to profile (can be a mix of direct and adjacent competitors)
2. **Your product** — what you do, so each profile can be compared against you
3. **Depth** — quick scan (key facts and positioning) or deep profile (full research including reviews, SEO, and content strategy)
4. **Focus areas** — any dimensions to prioritize, such as pricing, messaging, security or compliance claims, content strategy, or SEO strength

If the URLs and product context are clear, proceed without further questions.

---

## Core Principles

**Facts over opinions**
Every claim in a profile should trace back to a source — a page, a review, a metric. Label inferences clearly as inferences.

**Structured and comparable**
All profiles use the same template. Consistency matters more than exhaustiveness on any single profile — the value is in comparison.

**Current and dated**
Profiles are snapshots. Always note the date. Flag anything that looks stale (e.g., "pricing page appears unchanged since 2023").

**Honest**
Don't exaggerate a rival's weaknesses or downplay their strengths. Accurate profiles are useful profiles. If this research will feed public comparison pages, remember that AI engines and buyers verify claims — inaccurate comparison pages get caught and distrust the brand that made them.

## Research Process

### Phase 1 — Read the Competitor's Site

For each competitor, review and extract from these key pages:

| Page | What to extract |
|------|----------------|
| **Homepage** | Headline, subheadline, value proposition, primary CTA, social-proof claims, who they're speaking to |
| **Pricing** | Tiers, prices, what's included at each tier, billing options (monthly/annual), free tier or trial, enterprise pricing signals, pricing transparency |
| **Features / Product** | Feature categories, key capabilities, how they describe each, demo or screenshot signals |
| **About** | Founding story, team size, funding (if mentioned), mission, headquarters |
| **Customers / Case studies** | Named customers, logos, industries served, outcomes highlighted |
| **Integrations** | Count, key integration names, integration categories |
| **Security / Trust / Compliance** | Data-handling claims, certifications (SOC 2, HIPAA, GDPR, ISO), trust center, privacy page |
| **Changelog / What's new** | Release velocity, recent feature focus, product direction signals |

For each page, note the URL and the date accessed. Flag anything that seems outdated.

---

### Phase 2 — Organic and Market Presence

Gauge how much organic reach and authority each competitor has.

**With SEO / backlink tools** (Ahrefs, Semrush, Moz, or similar): pull domain authority score, estimated monthly organic traffic, number of referring domains, top-ranking pages by traffic, and organic keyword count.

**Without those tools**: approximate from public signals — how much content they publish and how frequently, whether their pages appear in the first page of results for obvious category queries, how established the domain looks, visible press mentions, and inbound links you can find. Label all of these as estimates.

The goal is a relative read — who's clearly ahead on organic, who's not yet investing in content — not false precision.

Also check **AI search presence**: ask ChatGPT and Perplexity "best [your category] tools" and "[their brand] vs [your brand]" and note whether they appear, and what's said.

---

### Phase 3 — Reviews and Customer Sentiment

Find each competitor on review platforms and in communities. This phase often surfaces the most useful intelligence — customers say things in reviews that never appear on a company's own site.

**Review platforms to check:** G2, Capterra, TrustRadius, App Store (if applicable), Google Reviews (for local or consumer products), Trustpilot

**Communities to check:** Reddit (relevant subreddits), Hacker News, product-specific forums or Slack groups, LinkedIn comments

**What to extract:**
- Overall rating and review count
- Recurring themes in positive reviews (what people love and why)
- Recurring themes in negative reviews (what frustrates people) — this is where positioning gaps live
- 3–5 representative verbatim quotes (both positive and critical)
- Any consistent feature requests or "I wish it had…" comments

---

### Phase 4 — Synthesize

Cross-reference what the company claims against what the evidence supports. If they claim "10,000 customers," does their organic footprint, review count, and team size support that scale? If the mismatch is significant, note it.

Then fill in the profile template for each competitor. Build the summary last, after all individual profiles are complete.

## Quick Scan vs. Deep Profile

**Quick scan** (default unless asked otherwise, or 5+ competitors):
- Pages to review: homepage + pricing only
- SEO: rough organic estimate only
- Reviews: skip or skim
- Output: At a Glance + Positioning + Pricing + one-line organic note

**Deep profile** (3 or fewer competitors, or explicitly requested):
- Pages to review: all key pages including about, customers, integrations, security, changelog
- SEO: best available data with labeled estimates
- Reviews: full sentiment extraction with representative quotes
- AI search: checked across ChatGPT and Perplexity
- Output: the full template above

---

## Handling Multiple Competitors

Pull the **same fields and metrics** for every competitor so profiles compare cleanly. Build the cross-competitor summary last.

If there are 10 or more competitors: profile the 5 closest (by product overlap and market similarity) first, note which were deprioritized and why, and offer to continue.

---

## Quality Checks and Guardrails

Before delivering any profile:

- [ ] Every claim has a source — page, review, or metric — noted in the Sources table
- [ ] All estimates are labeled as estimates (especially organic traffic and team size)
- [ ] The "last updated" date is included for any potentially stale data
- [ ] Strengths are acknowledged honestly — no artificially favorable comparisons
- [ ] Weaknesses are based on evidence (reviews, missing pages, complaints), not speculation
- [ ] The "Implications" section compares meaningfully to the user's actual product
- [ ] Any claim that will feed a public-facing comparison page is verified — not assumed
- [ ] The template structure is complete and consistent across all profiles

---

## Expected Output Format

Deliver:
1. One complete profile per competitor using the template above
2. The cross-competitor summary

If the depth is "quick scan," deliver abbreviated profiles (At a Glance + Positioning + Pricing only).

Always include the date and flag any estimates, stale data, or pages that couldn't be accessed.
