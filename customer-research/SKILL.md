# Customer Research

**Uncover what your customers actually think, say, and struggle with — so your messaging, product, and copy are grounded in evidence, not assumption.**

---

## What This Skill Does

This skill helps you conduct, analyze, and synthesize customer research into actionable insight: voice-of-customer language for copy, personas for targeting, jobs-to-be-done frameworks for positioning, and gap analysis for product and messaging decisions.

It works in two modes: analyzing research you already have (interviews, surveys, tickets, reviews), or going out to find research from public sources (communities, review sites, forums). Most projects use both.

---

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when you want to:

- Understand why customers buy, churn, or don't convert
- Find the language your customers use (to use in your own copy)
- Build accurate buyer personas or ICPs
- Validate or sharpen your positioning before launch
- Analyze interviews, surveys, support conversations, or reviews
- Find out what customers say about competitors

**Trigger phrases:** "customer research," "ICP research," "customer interviews," "survey analysis," "review mining," "voice of customer," "VOC," "build personas," "buyer persona," "jobs to be done," "JTBD," "what do customers say," "why are customers leaving," "why don't they convert," "Reddit mining," "G2 reviews"

## Before Starting

Ask two questions first, then follow up only as needed:

1. **What's the goal?** — sharpen messaging, build personas, find product gaps, understand why people buy or churn, or prepare for a launch?
2. **What do you already have?** — interview transcripts, survey results, support tickets, customer reviews, or nothing yet?

Then establish: which customer segment matters most, and what the product does (if not already clear).

## Two Modes of Research

| Mode | When to use it |
|------|---------------|
| **Mode 1 — Analyze existing assets** | You have transcripts, surveys, tickets, or reviews — extract the signal |
| **Mode 2 — Find research in public sources** | You need to gather language and insight from communities, review sites, and forums |

Most projects combine both. Establish which applies before proceeding.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Mode 1 — Analyzing Existing Research Assets

**By asset type, here's what to extract:**

**Interview and sales-call transcripts**
- What pain prompted them to look for a solution?
- What did they try before finding you?
- What was the trigger that made them act now?
- What language do they use for the problem?
- What objections came up?
- What alternatives did they consider?

**Survey results**
- Segment before concluding — don't average across all respondents; separate by customer tier, use case, or tenure
- Note where open-ended answers contradict multiple-choice responses — the verbatims usually win
- Focus on the ~20% of responses that contain the most concrete, specific feedback

**Support conversations**
- Mine for recurring complaints, confusion points, and "I wish it could…" phrasing
- Separate bugs from missing features from user confusion from wrong expectations
- Don't treat all tickets as equal signal — categorize first

**Win/loss interviews and churn notes**
- Wins: what finally tipped the decision? What nearly lost it?
- Losses and churn: was it price, fit, features, timing, relationship, or trust?
- Segment by reason — "product wasn't ready" and "too expensive" are different problems with different responses

**NPS responses**
- Detractors and passives contain more useful signal than promoters for improvement work
- Always pair the score with the verbatim comment — a 6 with a specific complaint beats a 9 with nothing

---

**Extraction framework — for every asset, pull these six dimensions:**

1. **Jobs to be done** — What outcome is the customer actually trying to achieve?
   - Functional job: the task (e.g., "send invoices faster")
   - Emotional job: how they want to feel (e.g., "confident, not anxious")
   - Social job: how they want to be perceived (e.g., "look competent to my boss")

2. **Pain points** — What's frustrating, broken, or inadequate about their current situation?
   - Prioritize pains mentioned unprompted and with emotional language

3. **Trigger events** — What changed that made them start looking?
   - Common triggers: a new hire, a missed deadline, a public failure, a new regulation, a competitor doing something

4. **Desired outcomes** — What does success look like in their words?
   - Capture exact quotes — paraphrasing loses the texture

5. **Language and vocabulary** — The literal words and phrases they use
   - "We were drowning in spreadsheets" is more useful than "inefficient manual process"
   - This language feeds directly into headline copy, ads, and landing pages

6. **Alternatives considered** — What else did they look at?
   - Including doing nothing, hiring someone, building it internally, or a competitor

---

**Synthesizing across assets:**

1. Cluster similar findings by theme across all sources
2. Score each theme: frequency (how often it appears) × intensity (how strongly it's felt)
3. Segment by customer profile — patterns often differ by company size, role, or use case
4. Pull 5–10 "money quotes" — verbatim quotes that best represent each theme
5. Flag contradictions — where customers say one thing but do another

---

**Quality guardrails for Mode 1:**

Label every insight with a confidence level before presenting it:

| Confidence | Criteria |
|-----------|----------|
| High | Theme appears in 3+ independent sources; mentioned unprompted; consistent across segments |
| Medium | Theme appears in 2 sources, or only when prompted, or limited to one segment |
| Low | Single source; could be an outlier; needs validation |

- Weight sources from the last 12 months more heavily — markets shift
- Correct for sample bias: online reviewers skew opinionated, tickets skew negative, technical communities skew skeptical
- Don't build a persona or draw a messaging conclusion from fewer than 5 independent data points per segment

### Mode 2 — Finding Research in Public Sources

Public communities are where customers speak without a filter. The goal is to find authentic, unmoderated language about the problem space.

**Where to look, by buyer type:**

| Buyer type | Primary sources |
|-----------|----------------|
| B2B / technical buyers | Reddit (role-specific subs), G2, Capterra, Hacker News, LinkedIn, Indie Hackers |
| Developer / engineering | r/devops, r/programming, Hacker News, Stack Overflow, Discord servers, GitHub issues |
| Small business / founders | r/entrepreneur, r/smallbusiness, Indie Hackers, Product Hunt discussions, Facebook groups |
| Consumer / B2C | App Store reviews (1–3 star), hobby/lifestyle subreddits, YouTube comments, TikTok comments |
| Regulated industries (legal, finance, healthcare) | Industry association forums, LinkedIn professional groups, niche subreddits, vertical review sites |
| Enterprise | LinkedIn, analyst reports, G2 enterprise filter, relevant job postings |

**Quick source selection guide:**
- Have a product category? → Start with G2/Capterra (yours + competitors')
- Need raw language? → Reddit and YouTube comments
- Need trigger events? → LinkedIn posts, job postings, "Ask HN" threads
- Need competitive intel? → Competitors' 4-star reviews (praise reveals expectations) and Product Hunt comments

---

**What to capture from each source:**

For every relevant piece of content, record:
- Source (platform, URL, date)
- The verbatim quote — exact words, not a paraphrase
- Context that prompted it
- Sentiment (positive / negative / neutral / frustrated)
- Theme tag (pain / trigger / outcome / alternative / language)
- Profile signals (role, company size, industry hints from the post)

---

### Persona Generation

Build personas from research, not imagination. Don't create a persona until you have at least 5–10 data points from a consistent segment.

**Persona template:**

```
## [Persona Name] — [Role / Title Range]

Profile
- Title range: [e.g., "Marketing Manager to VP of Marketing"]
- Organization type/size: [e.g., "50–500 employees, Series A–B SaaS"]
- Reports to: [if relevant]
- Team size managed: [if relevant]

Primary Job to Be Done
[One sentence: the outcome they're trying to achieve in their role]

Trigger Events
What causes them to start looking for a solution:
- [trigger 1]
- [trigger 2]

Top Pains (in their words where possible)
1. [pain]
2. [pain]
3. [pain]

Desired Outcomes
- [what success looks like to them]
- [how they measure it]
- [how it makes them look to others]

Objections and Fears
- [what makes them hesitate]

Alternatives They Consider
- [competitor / DIY / do nothing / hire someone]

Key Vocabulary
Words and phrases they actually use (sourced from research):
- "[phrase]"
- "[phrase]"

How to Reach Them
- Channels: [where they spend time]
- Content they consume: [formats and topics]
- Communities or voices they trust: [specific names if known]
```

**Persona anti-patterns to avoid:**
- Don't average across segments — a persona for everyone represents no one
- Don't invent details — leave a field blank rather than make it up
- Don't make them cute ("Marketing Mary") unless your team finds it genuinely useful
- Do revisit quarterly as your market and product evolve

## Deliverable Options

Before generating output, ask which format the user needs:

| Deliverable | Best for |
|-------------|---------|
| Research synthesis report | Themes, quotes, implications — the standard output |
| VOC quote bank | Organized verbatim phrases for use in copy |
| 1–3 personas | Buyer archetypes for targeting decisions |
| Jobs-to-be-done map | Functional, emotional, social jobs by segment |
| Competitive intelligence summary | What customers say about rivals vs. you |
| Research gap analysis | What you still don't know and how to find it |

---

## Quality Checks and Guardrails

- [ ] Every insight has a source — never present findings without saying where they came from
- [ ] Confidence levels are labeled (High / Medium / Low) — no false certainty
- [ ] Sample bias is acknowledged — who isn't represented in this data?
- [ ] Verbatim quotes are exact — paraphrases are labeled as such
- [ ] Personas are built from at least 5 data points — not invented
- [ ] Contradictions are flagged, not glossed over
- [ ] Recency is noted — insights from 3 years ago carry less weight
- [ ] The deliverable format matches the stated goal — don't produce a persona when what's needed is a quote bank
